RAMAYANA The Eternal Epic

As composed by the sage Valmiki

I

The Kingdom
of Ayodhya

In the fertile valley of the river Sarayu stood the magnificent city of Ayodhya — "the unconquerable." Ruled by the noble King Dasharatha of the Solar dynasty, it was a kingdom where righteousness flowed like the sacred river itself.

Four princes blessed the royal household: the virtuous Rama, the courageous Lakshmana, the devoted Bharata, and the valiant Shatrughna. Of all, Rama shone like the sun — gentle as the moon, fierce as fire, deep as the ocean.

King Dasharatha Prince Rama Lakshmana Bharata
II

The Swayamvara
of Sita

In the resplendent court of Mithila, King Janaka proclaimed the Swayamvara of his daughter Sita, born of the sacred earth. Only the warrior who could lift, string, and bend the immense, divine bow of Lord Shiva—the Pinaka—would win her hand in marriage.

Countless mighty kings and arrogant princes tried, but failed to even move the celestial weapon. Then, stepping forth with serene majesty, Prince Rama lifted the bow with absolute ease. As he strung it, the mighty bow snapped with a thunderous crack that echoed across the three worlds, trembling the very cosmos. With absolute devotion, Sita approached and placed the varmala (victory garland) around his neck, as the heavens showered flowers in joyous celebration.

Sita — Daughter of Earth King Janaka Rama — The Chosen
III

The Exile

Bound by an oath, King Dasharatha yielded to Queen Kaikeyi’s cruel demands: the banishment of Rama to the untamed wilderness for fourteen years, and the coronation of Bharata in his stead. The opulent gates of Ayodhya closed, while the endless dark forest opened its jaws to swallow the prince of light.

Unyielding in their devotion, Sita and Lakshmana followed him into exile, exchanging silks for tree bark. Behind them, a kingdom wept. Ahead of them lay a path shrouded in thorns and prophecy.

Kaikeyi — The Scheming Queen Rama · Sita · Lakshmana
IV

Life in the
Forest

Deep within the sprawling shadows of the Dandaka forest, Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana forged an unshakeable bond with the wilderness. They built a modest hermitage at Panchavati, beside the shimmering Godavari river. Among ancient, towering trees and glowing fireflies, they protected meditating ascetics from the terrifying demons that haunted the groves.

Yet, the tranquility of exile was not meant to last. The monstrous demoness Shurpanakha—sister to the cruel king Ravana—stumbled into their sacred grove. Smitten but spurned, she attacked Sita, leading Lakshmana to punish her severely. Enraged, she fled to Lanka, dragging the wheels of fate toward an inevitable war.

Sages of the Forest Shurpanakha
V

The Golden
Deer

Ravana enlisted the demon sorcerer Maricha to take the form of a magical golden deer — a creature of impossible beauty, its hide shimmering like molten starlight. It appeared before their hermitage, and Sita, enchanted, asked Rama to capture it.

Lakshmana warned it was a demon's trick, but Sita's heart was set. Rama pursued the deer deep into the forest. Mortally wounded, Maricha cried out in Rama's voice: "O Lakshmana! O Sita!" The trap was set. The forest grew dark.

Maricha — The Golden Illusion Ravana's Design
VI

The Abduction
of Sita

With both brothers away, Ravana approached in the guise of a sage. The great ten-headed demon king of Lanka cast off his disguise and seized Sita, dragging her into his flying chariot Pushpaka. Sita cried out for Rama across the three worlds.

The noble vulture Jatayu, faithful to Dasharatha, attacked Ravana valiantly — but the demon severed his wings. Dying, Jatayu held on only long enough to tell Rama what had happened. The darkest chapter had begun.

Ravana — The Ten-Headed King Jatayu — The Faithful Eagle
VII

Hanuman
Meets Rama

On the Rishyamukha mountain, the monkey king Sugriva lived in exile, aided by his general — the mighty Hanuman, son of the Wind God himself. When Rama and Lakshmana arrived, Hanuman recognized the divine light in Rama's eyes.

In the instant their eyes met, Hanuman fell to his knees. He had found the Lord he had been born to serve. In that meeting of devotee and deity, a friendship was forged that would shake the cosmos. Sugriva and Rama made a sacred alliance.

Hanuman — Son of the Wind Sugriva — Monkey King
VIII

The Leap
to Lanka

The ocean stretched one hundred yojanas to Lanka — an impossible distance. But Hanuman was the son of Vayu, the Wind God. Recalling his divine power, he grew to the size of a mountain, compressed the earth with his feet, and hurled himself across the sky.

Sea monsters rose to stop him. Surasa, mother of serpents, opened her mouth wide — Hanuman made himself tiny, entered and exited in an instant. The very ocean trembled in awe. He landed on the shores of Lanka like a thunderbolt from heaven.

Hanuman — Mountain-Sized The One Hundred Yojana Leap
IX

The Burning
of Lanka

Hanuman found Sita in the Ashoka grove, a prisoner but unbroken. He delivered Rama's ring and her faith was renewed. But Ravana's forces captured him and sentenced him to death — then changed it to setting his tail on fire.

They did not know the tail of the Wind God's son is infinite. Hanuman let them wrap his tail in cloth and set it ablaze. Then he broke free, and using his own burning tail as a torch, leapt from palace to palace, turning Lanka's golden city to cinders. He returned to Rama with news of Sita.

Lanka in Flames Hanuman — The Invincible
X

The Great
Battle

Rama built a bridge of rocks across the ocean — each stone inscribed with his name, floating by the power of devotion. The vast army of Vanaras crossed to Lanka. The battle that followed shook the three worlds for days and nights without ceasing.

Ravana's generals fell one by one. When his son Indrajit mortally wounded Lakshmana, Hanuman flew to the Himalayas and carried back an entire mountain bearing the life-restoring herb Sanjeevani. No matter what demons they sent, Rama's army did not break.

The Vanara Army Indrajit · Kumbhakarna
XI

Ravana's
Defeat

Finally Rama and Ravana stood face to face. They fought for days. Every time Rama severed Ravana's head, a new one grew back. Then the sage Agastya revealed the secret: Rama must use the Brahmastra — a weapon forged from the elements of creation.

Rama took the divine arrow, chanted the sacred mantras, and released it. It pierced Ravana's chest where his immortality resided. The sky fell silent. The ten-headed demon who had terrorized the three worlds for millennia fell — and light returned to the cosmos.

The Brahmastra — Divine Arrow Dharma Restored
XII

Return to
Ayodhya

Fourteen years of exile ended. Rama, Sita, and Lakshmana returned to Ayodhya in the Pushpaka Vimana — the divine flying chariot — as the entire kingdom waited below. Bharata had ruled only as regent, keeping Rama's sandals on the throne as a symbol of true kingship.

The people of Ayodhya lit oil lamps in every doorway, on every rooftop, along every path — ten thousand points of light driving away fourteen years of darkness. Heaven scattered flowers. This was the first Diwali — the Festival of Lights — and it has been celebrated every year since.

Diwali — Festival of Lights Rama Rajya — The Perfect Kingdom
